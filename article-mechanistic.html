Inside the Black Box: What Mechanistic Interpretability Has Found So Far

YT
Yuki Tanaka
May 15, 2026
18 min read
Papers

Neural networks are commonly criticized as black boxes: systems that translate inputs into outputs through billions of parameters, yet yield no clarity on their internal decision-making rules. **Mechanistic Interpretability** seeks to change this by reverse-engineering network weights into human-understandable algorithms.

Instead of inspecting entire networks, researchers dissect components into sub-circuits. These circuits consist of specific neurons and attention heads that collaborate to perform discrete sub-tasks, like detecting uppercase letters or tracking indirect objects.

Neural Circuits and Superposition

A core challenge in mechanistic interpretability is **superposition**: the tendency of neural networks to represent more features than they have dimensions by mapping multiple concepts to linear combinations of the same neuron weights.

When features are sparse, a model can compress them into a lower-dimensional space. By identifying these active coordinates, researchers can reconstruct **circuits**: subgraphs of nodes that govern specific features.

Mechanistic De-obfuscation

By mapping these circuits, researchers have found that models learn discrete mathematical structures. For instance, in attention layers, models form **induction heads**: two-head circuits that search for repetitions of previous token patterns. These circuits represent the core mechanism behind in-context learning.
