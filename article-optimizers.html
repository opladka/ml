AdamW, Muon, and Why the Optimizer Still Matters

LJ
Li Jian
April 28, 2026
12 min read
Tutorials

In deep learning, data sets and scaling parameters receive massive attention. Yet, the algorithm guiding weights down the high-dimensional loss surface—the **Optimizer**—remains one of the most critical variables in training velocity.

From simple Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) to Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation) and its weight-decay corrected variant AdamW, optimizers have evolved to handle flat regions, saddle points, and noisy training landscapes.

Adaptive Rates: From SGD to AdamW

SGD applies a fixed learning rate to the gradient. If the gradient is steep, the step is large; if it is flat, steps crawl.

Adam tracks the running exponential average of both the gradient (first moment) and the squared gradient (second moment), scaling learning rates dynamically per parameter. AdamW corrects a minor mistake in early Adam integrations by decoupling weight decay from the gradient update step directly.

Enter Muon: Orthogonalized Gradient Descent

Recent experiments introduced **Muon**, a new optimizer designed specifically for training the Feed-Forward networks of Transformers. Instead of updating coordinates independently, Muon computes the gradient matrix and applies **Newton-Schulz iteration** to output an orthogonalized update.

By keeping updates orthogonal, Muon prevents parameter values from collapsing into narrow valleys and speeds up initial convergence.
